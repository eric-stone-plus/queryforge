# QueryForge Pitch Deck Content Plan

## Deck Strategy

**Positioning:** QueryForge frees data analysts from repetitive business requests. Analysts define trusted data and metrics once; business teams then self-serve through natural language to fetch data, create dashboards, analyze anomalies, and receive decision recommendations.

**Audience:** Hackathon judges, early investors, and business decision-makers. The deck should feel like a business pitch, not a technical review.

**Story arc:** pain -> solution -> product -> proof -> business -> ask.

**Core value to repeat throughout:** analyst efficiency plus business self-service.

**Narrative rule:** Every slide should answer one of two questions:

- How does QueryForge give analysts time back?
- How does QueryForge help business teams move without waiting?

## Slide 1: QueryForge

**Title:** QueryForge

**Core message:** Free data analysts from repetitive requests, and give business teams a faster way to make data-backed decisions.

**Visual elements:**

- Dark corporate hero slide with the QueryForge name large and centered-left.
- Subheadline: "Set up once. Self-serve every day."
- Right side: a clean workflow image showing Analyst Setup -> Business Question -> Dashboard / Anomaly / Recommendation.
- Small proof strip at bottom: "Tested on 10K orders across 8 regions and 20 categories."

**Speaker emphasis:** Open with the operational pain, not the tool. Analysts are high-leverage employees, but too much of their week is consumed by repeat adjustments and reporting requests.

## Slide 2: The Pain

**Title:** Business Questions Move Faster Than Analytics Teams

**Core message:** Business teams constantly need metric changes, added fields, dashboard edits, anomaly checks, and recommendations. Analysts become the manual bridge between every question and every answer.

**Visual elements:**

- Left: business team request cards stacking up: "Add region split", "Refresh category view", "Explain the spike", "Compare this segment", "Update dashboard".
- Center: bottleneck icon labeled "Analyst queue".
- Right: delayed decisions: "Pricing", "Inventory", "Marketing", "Operations".
- Use subtle red or amber accents only on friction points.

**Speaker emphasis:** The problem is not lack of data. The problem is that useful data still requires too much analyst intervention for everyday business questions.

## Slide 3: The Cost

**Title:** Repetitive Requests Drain Analyst Capacity

**Core message:** Analysts should be improving business understanding, not repeatedly rebuilding the same views for slightly different questions.

**Visual elements:**

- Two-column comparison:
  - "What analysts are asked to do": adjust metrics, add fields, revise dashboards, explain recurring anomalies.
  - "What analysts should focus on": define trusted metrics, uncover drivers, improve decision quality, guide strategy.
- A simple capacity bar showing repetitive requests taking the largest share, without using speculative numbers.
- Footer line: "Every repeated request is a missed opportunity for higher-value analysis."

**Speaker emphasis:** This frames analyst efficiency as a business outcome: faster cycles, less rework, better use of scarce talent.

## Slide 4: The Solution

**Title:** Set Up Once, Self-Serve Repeatedly

**Core message:** QueryForge lets analysts create trusted business-ready foundations once, then business users ask follow-up questions in plain language.

**Visual elements:**

- Clean hub-and-spoke diagram:
  - Center: "Trusted metrics and data context".
  - Left input: "Analyst setup".
  - Right outputs: "Data answers", "Dashboards", "Anomaly explanations", "Recommendations".
- Use checkmarks for "consistent definitions", "business-friendly answers", and "fewer repeat requests".

**Speaker emphasis:** The key shift is from analyst-as-ticket-handler to analyst-as-enabler. Business users get autonomy while analysts retain consistency and governance.

## Slide 5: The Product Experience

**Title:** From Question To Decision In One Flow

**Core message:** Business users can ask, explore, visualize, and act without turning every follow-up into a new analyst request.

**Visual elements:**

- Four-step product flow:
  1. Ask: "Why did orders drop in the East region?"
  2. Fetch: relevant order and regional data appears.
  3. Analyze: anomaly drivers are surfaced.
  4. Decide: recommended next actions are shown.
- Mock product screen with a dark sidebar, a natural-language input bar, a dashboard preview, and a recommendation panel.
- Keep the UI mock restrained and credible, like an enterprise productivity product.

**Speaker emphasis:** Show business self-service as more than asking questions. It includes exploration, visual reporting, issue diagnosis, and decision support.

## Slide 6: Proof With Real Business Data

**Title:** Proved On A Realistic Commerce Dataset

**Core message:** QueryForge was demonstrated on 10K orders spanning 8 regions and 20 categories, showing it can handle realistic business complexity.

**Visual elements:**

- Three proof tiles:
  - "10K orders" for volume.
  - "8 regions" for geographic segmentation.
  - "20 categories" for product complexity.
- Map-style regional chips, category grid, and order trend line.
- Demo question examples:
  - "Which region is underperforming?"
  - "What changed by category?"
  - "Where is the anomaly?"
  - "What should the team do next?"

**Speaker emphasis:** The dataset proves the pitch in business terms: multiple regions, many categories, enough orders to create realistic patterns, and questions that mirror daily operations.

## Slide 7: Value For Analysts And Business Teams

**Title:** One Product, Two Compounding Wins

**Core message:** Analysts gain leverage; business users gain independence. Together, the company gets faster decisions with more consistent data use.

**Visual elements:**

- Split screen:
  - Left: "Analyst efficiency" with icons for fewer repeat tickets, reusable metrics, more time for strategic analysis.
  - Right: "Business self-service" with icons for instant answers, dashboard creation, anomaly explanation, decision recommendations.
- Center bridge: "Trusted context keeps both sides aligned."
- Use a muted green accent for positive outcomes.

**Speaker emphasis:** This is the slide where the core value becomes unmistakable. QueryForge is not just a convenience layer; it changes the operating rhythm between analysts and business teams.

## Slide 8: Business Opportunity

**Title:** Built For Teams Where Data Demand Outruns Analyst Capacity

**Core message:** The first buyers are teams with frequent reporting requests, recurring operational decisions, and limited analyst bandwidth.

**Visual elements:**

- Target customer segments:
  - Ecommerce and retail operations.
  - Growth and marketing teams.
  - Revenue and customer success teams.
  - Finance and operations leaders.
- Wedge diagram:
  - Start: internal reporting and self-service analytics.
  - Expand: dashboards, anomaly workflows, decision recommendations.
  - Grow: company-wide decision workspace.
- Business model note: "Team-based subscriptions with analyst-led setup."

**Speaker emphasis:** Keep this practical. QueryForge starts where the pain is already visible: teams repeatedly asking analysts for decision support.

## Slide 9: The Ask

**Title:** Help Us Turn Analyst Bottlenecks Into Business Self-Service

**Core message:** QueryForge is ready for pilots with teams that face frequent data requests and want faster decision cycles.

**Visual elements:**

- Three clear ask cards:
  - "Pilot teams": validate weekly business workflows.
  - "Design partners": shape setup, governance, and adoption.
  - "Hackathon support": feedback, introductions, and judging consideration.
- End with the tagline: "Analysts define the truth. Business teams move on it."
- Include contact / QR placeholder if needed.

**Speaker emphasis:** Close by returning to the human problem. QueryForge gives analysts their time back and gives business teams a direct path from question to decision.

## Design Direction

**Overall feel:** Dark corporate, polished, credible, and restrained. The deck should look like it belongs in a boardroom or enterprise sales meeting, not a consumer app launch.

**Palette:**

- Background: near-black navy `#0B1220`.
- Panel surfaces: deep slate `#111827`.
- Primary text: off-white `#F8FAFC`.
- Secondary text: cool gray `#CBD5E1`.
- Primary accent: teal `#14B8A6`.
- Success accent: green `#22C55E`.
- Warning accent: amber `#F59E0B`.
- Friction accent: red `#EF4444`, used sparingly.

**Typography:**

- Titles: bold, clean sans-serif, 34-42 pt.
- Body: medium-weight sans-serif, 16-22 pt.
- Data callouts: large numeric type, 30-40 pt.
- Avoid decorative fonts, heavy gradients, cartoon visuals, robot imagery, and glossy synthetic patterns.

**Layout principles:**

- One core message per slide.
- Use diagrams, product mocks, and data proof tiles instead of dense paragraphs.
- Keep slide backgrounds dark and stable; use accents only to guide attention.
- Make the 10K / 8 / 20 proof visible as a recurring confidence marker across the deck.
- Use consistent icon style: thin-line, enterprise-friendly, monochrome with accent highlights.

## How To Keep The Core Value Visible

**Analyst efficiency:**

- Repeat the phrase "fewer repeat requests" on pain, solution, and value slides.
- Show analysts moving from queue work to strategic work.
- Use visuals that turn many repeated requests into one reusable foundation.
- Avoid framing analysts as replaced; frame them as amplified and more strategic.

**Business self-service:**

- Show business users asking natural-language questions and getting usable outputs.
- Make outputs concrete: data tables, dashboards, anomaly explanations, and decision recommendations.
- Emphasize speed and independence without promising exact time savings.
- Show business teams as enabled by analyst-defined trust, not operating in isolation.

## Real Data Proof Strategy

Use the 10K orders, 8 regions, and 20 categories as credibility markers in three places:

- Slide 1: proof strip under the positioning.
- Slide 6: dedicated proof slide with dataset scope and demo questions.
- Slide 7 or 9: reminder that the product value was demonstrated on a realistic operating dataset.

The proof should connect to business questions:

- Regions prove the product handles geographic comparison.
- Categories prove the product handles product mix and segmentation.
- Orders prove the product handles transaction-level operational reality.
- Anomaly analysis proves the product goes beyond static reporting into decision support.

## Recommended 5-Minute Pitch Flow

1. Open with the analyst bottleneck and the repeated-request loop.
2. Explain why the cost is lost analyst leverage and slower business decisions.
3. Introduce QueryForge as the reusable foundation for business self-service.
4. Walk through the product flow from question to dashboard to recommendation.
5. Prove realism with 10K orders, 8 regions, and 20 categories.
6. Show the dual value: analysts regain capacity, business teams gain autonomy.
7. Describe the first customer wedge and business model.
8. Close with the pilot and design partner ask.
